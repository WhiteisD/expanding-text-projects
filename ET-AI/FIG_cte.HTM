The Inventing Process
    Have an idea
    - Learn to notice when you have an idea
    - Write it down! (See "Inventor's Notebook" below.)
    - Make an effort at it
    Add to Inventor's Notebook
    - Available several places, including www.BookFactory.com
    - Must have bound pages that can't be added or removed.
    - Write down invention ideas, and interactions with others
    - Get it witnessed frequently, by a party with no financial interest in your efforts.
    Assess its value
    - This is a tough one.
    - Inventors cannot see their own inventions with much perspective.
    - Search on Google and USPTO web site.
    - Walk around Wal-Mart.

    Build a mock-up
    - Scotch tape and baling wire will do to start.
    - It doesn't have to work.
    Choose an NDA
    - Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)
    - Watch for "hidden gotchas"
    - I like David Pressman's; a photocopy from his book.
    Get some help
    - Get everyone you tell about it to sign your NDA--build the habit.
    - But don't be overly cautious - your idea is much more likely to be ignored than stolen.

    File a provisional patent
    - Try to make it as close to a real utility patent as you can make it.
    - But don't worry if it is not perfect, just get it filed.
    - It only costs $100.
    - You will learn a great deal about the process and you will clarify what it good and what is weak about your invention.
    Write your business plan
    - Some directions you might take, you will always be 100% blocked without a business plan.
    - Many more stupid ideas with detailed plans get money than great ideas with no plan.
    Build a working prototype
    - Local inventors groups can help you find engineers.
    - Again, wander the isles of stores, look for already existing products to start from.
    - Having it will help you stay believing in your idea.
    Publicize your invention
    - Local newspapers are ready for your call.
    - TV news organizations are too.
    - Try the invention TV shows.
    Choose: License it
    - License it: a tough game to play, it is a closed community
    - Manufacturing in your basement: can help you start on a shoestring
    - Selling a small number of units can get you a better licensing deal.
    Identify potential licensors
    - What is already on the shelf?
    - Learn about those companies; do your research.
    - Product > USPTO.gov > Inventor > Licensor info
    Reach the decision maker
    - Have the web site ready
    - Practice (20 second?) intro
    - Call them up!
    Get NDA signed
    - Understand why companies don't want to signs NDAs.
    - You have to tell them enough to get them interested, but not so much they don't need to hear your full pitch.
    - You should know your NDA well. Hesitate before accepting one of theirs.

    Choose: Build and sell it yourself
    - Build some product, build a web site, sell some.
    - Can get you a better licensing deal if it works out.
    - Can bring you back to reality if you never sell one.
    Local resources
    - Prototyping firms
    - Manufacturers
    - Inventors groups
    - Entrepreneurs groups

    File patent
    - Costly.
    - Do as much on your own as you can.
    - Write your own first draft of the text at least.
    - Do the best drawings you can.
    Strike a deal
    - Understand that there is no mysterious magic to negotiations like this. You can do it.
    - Say, "this sounds like what I'm looking for, but I can't agree until my lawyer looks at it."
    - Follow a couple of other basic rules and you can get far enough, so you know it is now a good time to bring in a lawyer with negotiating experience.

    Do it again!
    - Don't worry if the first one doesn't work out.
    - Now you've learned a great deal about the process, and you've got the bug.
    - Do it again, only better!
